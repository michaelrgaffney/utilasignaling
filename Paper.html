Child Signals of Need in Utila, Honduras: An Exploratory Study

Introduction

Despite the complexity of childhood and adolescence and their importance to adult outcomes, the behavioral strategies of children are often overlooked, especially when it comes to how they elicit support from parents and other caretakers (Gottlieb 2000; Wells 2003; Hirschfeld 2002). To gain support, children use signals to communicate their desires to parents. Infant signals of need, particularly infant cries, have been extensively studied in a range of populations (Barr et al. 1991; Fouts, Hewlett, and Lamb 2005; Konner 1974; Kruger and Konner 2010; Kushnick 2006; Meehan, Hagen, and Hewlett 2017; Müller 2021; Tronick, Morelli, and Winn 1987), yet the full range of potential child signals of need, which likely extend beyond cries, has never been investigated in children of all ages. In particular, if and when children signal need with costly signals (Grafen 1990; Spence 1973; Zahavi 1975) has rarely been investigated (for one exception, see, Syme and Hagen 2023).

In this paper, we use exploratory analysis on child signaling data from Utila Honduras to examine the hypothesis that children have evolved strategies to tailor their signals of need to 1) their valuation of a given resource, 2) the extent of conflicts of interest with caretakers, and 3) the presence of existing information about a child’s need or potential to benefit from a resource so that they can credibly convey they are worth additional investment. Such strategies can lead to some combination of verbal requests for more support, facial sadness, crying, withdrawal, temper tantrums and self-harm, all of which may be effective in certain circumstances and are sometimes successful in eliciting more support from caretakers, despite a child’s low status and formidability.

Interdependence and investment within the family

Throughout human evolutionary history, individuals often found themselves in situations in which they received fitness-relevant benefits from others that could not easily be replaced (Apicella et al. 2012; Apicella and Silk 2019; Hill et al. 2011; Tooby and Cosmides 1996). In these instances, natural selection is expected to have favored those who provided support to others if 1) such help meant one could expect to receive fitness benefits from those helped in the future and 2) the costs of providing help were outweighed by the benefits to the helper on average (André 2010; Hamilton 1964; Tooby and Cosmides 1996; Trivers 1971).

Care motivated by a high degree of fitness interdependence between offspring and at least one parent is seen in many animals (Clutton-Brock 1991; Webb et al. 1999). However, humans are a uniquely interdependent species among the living primates (Tomasello et al. 2012) in that one’s fitness prospects depend greatly on a network of kin and other social allies (Apicella and Silk 2019). This need for others is most intense with children, who are born highly altricial and require many years of investment to develop to the point where they can obtain enough food to survive on their own and develop the social skills needed to succeed in their communities (Kaplan, Hooper, and Gurven 2009; Rosenberg 2021; Schniter et al. 2015). Much of this investment comes from parents (Kramer and Veile 2018), However, other adults like grandparents, aunts, uncles, and non-relatives have also been found to provide significant investment in children in many societies (Kramer and Veile 2018; Sear and Mace 2008), something which was likely favored due to a combination of indirect fitness benefits, reciprocity, or reputation management (Gurven et al. 2000) + (Schniter Preprint (permission needed). Children also often serve as alloparents, helping to raise their younger siblings or provide for them through food preparation or collection (Hrdy 2009; Meehan 2009; Sear and Mace 2008), despite still requiring substantial amounts of investment themselves (Kramer 2018).

Conflicts of interest within the family

Although the fitness prospects of both children and familial caretakers are largely intertwined, evolutionary theory predicts that disagreements about the amount of investment from parents will be ubiquitous (Fouts, Hewlett, and Lamb 2005; Godfray 1995; Godfray and Parker 1992; Trivers 1974). Most resources relevant to fitness outcomes are limited so that one unit of a resource invested in one area cannot be used in another. In response to this basic fact of life, humans, like other organisms, are expected to have evolved to efficiently allocate resources between somatic effort (i.e., growth and maintenance) and reproductive effort (i.e., mating and parenting effort) (Daly and Wilson 1983), something which often pits the fitness interests of parents against the fitness interests of offspring.

Conflicts of interest within families are largely structured by the likelihood an individual shares their genes with another. As a sexually reproducing species, humans have a 50% chance of passing down a given gene to each child. Each child, however, shares more genes through descent with themselves (100%) than with their full siblings (50% on average), half-siblings (25% on average), and stepsiblings (0%). Due to this asymmetry, children are often expected to attempt to elicit more investment from parents than is optimal from the perspective of the parent, while parents are expected to attempt to invest less than is optimal for each child in order to invest in their other children, whom parents are expected to value identically, all else being equal (Trivers 1974).

For the same reason, siblings also often find themselves in conflicts of interest with one another, with each being expected to prefer more resources for themself than others. Sibling competition is evident across taxa and can range from relatively benign forms like strong begging competition in chicks (Caro, West, and Griffin 2016) to siblicide in birds, mammals, and sharks (Salmon and Hehman 2014). In humans, sibling competition is largely estimated by counts of siblings, and large numbers of siblings have been associated with decreased growth (Hadley et al. 2011; Edward H. Hagen, Barrett, and Price 2006; Salmon and Hehman 2014), survival (Lawson, Alvergne, and Gibson 2012; Meij et al. 2009; Strassmann 2011), and reproductive success (Gibson and Gurmu 2011; Mace 1996) in multiple populations, highlighting the likely significance of sibling competition for resources, even if this does not always manifest with behavioral conflict (Bateson 1994).

Conflicts of interest between children and parents may also involve how much children invest in the household. As previously mentioned, each child is expected to prefer greater investment in oneself than in one’s siblings, while parents, who are equally related to all their children, should often be expected to favor a more even distribution (Trivers 1974). This can lead to conflict with older children who might prefer to spend their time in ways which are likely to result in future direct fitness benefits (e.g., finding romantic partners or forming strong bonds with friends) rather than taking care of their siblings or acquiring or preparing food (Lowe 2003; Syme and Hagen 2023). Conflicts of interest over investment in the household also appear to extend to when a child starts having their own children (Kushnick 2023; Emlen 1995), with parents potentially benefiting from delaying the reproductive careers of certain offspring in order to gain more care for their other children (Flinn 1989; Emlen and Wrege 1992) or speeding up a child’s departure from the household if the child’s presence limits how much caretakers can invest in other children they deem more worthy of investment (Kushnick 2023).

Signaling

In order to elicit more support, children often use signals. Signals are a key source of information for individuals in social species and are commonly used to indicate one’s value as a mate, one’s formidability to competitors, or one’s worthiness of investment to parents and other social partners (Caro, West, and Griffin 2016; Maynard-Smith and Harper 2004; Preuschoft and Schaik 2000; Setchell and Jean Wickings 2005; Zahavi 1975). Signals differ from cues in that signals were favored by natural selection due to their ability to alter the behavior or perceptions of others in ways that benefit the signaler, whereas cues are pieces of information organisms use to influence their behavior or physiological state that did not evolve for communicative effect (Maynard Smith and Harper 1995). Crying is likely a signal of need1, for instance, while bleeding would be a cue of need.

When conflicts of interest over investment exist, there may be much to gain from deception (i.e., exaggerating or completely fabricating one’s quality or need) so that one can receive the benefits that come from being seen as having a particular quality without actually doing so (Zahavi 1993). Although the risks of being deceived can be substantial, signal receivers may still benefit from paying attention to signals so long as the information is credible enough to inform one’s behavior in ways that are adaptive on average (Bliege Bird, Smith, and Bird 2001; Grafen 1990). These benefits coupled with the likelihood of strong selection against individuals who respond to fake signals results in the expectation that signals within species will often need to benefit both signaler and receiver to persist evolutionary (Hasson 1994; Maynard-Smith and Harper 2004).

Credible signals[2] can evolve and be maintained in multiple ways. Despite reasonable expectations that such circumstances will be rare (Zahavi 1993), signals can be produced and trusted in instances when there are no conflicts of interest over what information is conveyed (Guilford and Dawkins 1995; Maynard Smith and Harper 1995). They may also arise when signals cannot be faked (i.e., indexical signals) or in instances when there are greater net payoffs to signalers who have the quality being signaled to a greater extent than others (Maynard Smith and Harper 1995). Although the latter can arise through differential benefits (Penn and Számadó 2020; Tiokhin 2016) or a combination of differential costs and benefits (Bergstrom preprint), more attention has been paid to the importance of differential costs alone, with costly signaling being a possible pathway to credible signaling whenever signals are prohibitively costly for those who might fake the signal but still worth making for those with the underlying trait (Maynard-Smith and Harper 2004).

Credible signals work by revealing private information about one’s qualities to others. In the context of child signals of need, a child has access to information about their internal state, which is unavailable to others, that allows them to estimate their valuation of additional investment. By signaling in a credible way, the child reveals their resource valuation to potential caretakers who may be skeptical of the child’s claims due to the common conflicts of interest described above. With the information revealed by the signal, caretakers should generally be more likely to provide additional support due to their fitness interest in the child’s wellbeing. When this is the case, all parties are better off than they would be without the signal, with caretakers benefitting from the revelation of fitness-relevant information and the child benefitting from the increased likelihood of a beneficial response as long as the benefits outweigh the costs of the signal to child and parent.

However, even if a child was completely successful in conveying their needs to caretakers, it may not be enough to result in a beneficial response. This is because belief in one’s child’s need is not the only factor determining the form and amount of support given by parents. Instead, parents are expected to weigh the costs and benefits of support with other investment options (Daly and Wilson 1987, 1983). Ancestrally, this likely often meant evaluating the viability of the child as well as how much a particular resource benefited one child relative to other children or future children (Clutton-Brock 1991; Daly and Wilson 1983; Dawkins 1976; Edward H. Hagen 1999). The former is thought to have resulted in children having to demonstrate their vigor early in life (Soltis 2004), while the latter is expected to result in context-specific support (Beaulieu and Bugental 2008; Frankenhuis and Amir 2022). For example, one unit of a resource is often expected to be worth more to a newborn than an adolescent, favoring that investment flowing to the newborn all else being equal. However, if resources are in short supply, parents may benefit more from investing more in older children as they are closer to reaching adulthood and have higher reproductive value (Daly and Wilson 1983). Additionally, a parent may also decide against investing in a child despite deeming investment in a child worthwhile if time is not right (e.g., if a mother preferred to breastfeed during rest rather than foraging) (Altmann 1980). When this is the case, outward conflicts may exaggerate the extent of a conflict of interest and involve parents teaching children ways to behave that benefit them both (Altmann 1980).

Crying

Crying is the most studied way by which children may signal need. Crying is a human universal (Gracanin, Bylsma, and Vingerhoets 2018) with few suspected non-signaling functions outside of the role vocal cries may play in the development of vocalization (Wells 2003) and the functions tears have in terms of cleaning, protecting, and lubricating the eye (Murube 2009; Sznycer, Gracanin, and Lieberman, n.d.). Hunger, pain, anger, fear, and separation are all common causes of crying (Vingerhoets 2013; Wells 2003), although tear production also often happens with joyful events (Sznycer, Gracanin, and Lieberman, n.d.; Vingerhoets 2013; Zickfeld et al., n.d.).

Infant cries have been found to result in increased amounts of care, resources, and attention they receive and increased proximity to parents (Lin and McFatter 2012). For example, Karo Batak infants who cried more were breastfed more, and the length of crying bouts predicted the time mothers breastfed (Kushnick 2006). Similarly, !Kung San mothers frequently respond to crying infants by providing comfort and breastfeeding access, with comforting behaviors, in general, being far more common than one’s meant to restrict infant behavior (Kruger and Konner 2010). Although this study found mothers were the most common responders, siblings, fathers, and other adults frequently stepped in so that 88% of crying bouts were responded to, with most of the cries not responded to lasting under ten seconds. Similar responsiveness has also been reported among the Aka (Hewlett et al. 1998), Mbendjele hunter-gatherers (Chaudhary, Salali, and Swanepoel 2024), the Efe (Tronick, Morelli, and Winn 1987), and the Ache, among whom young children have been described as “win[ning] most conflicts with parents simply by crying and whining” (Kaplan and Dove 1987). Studies examining the potential for long-term benefits are rare and difficult to achieve; however, during a time of high infant mortality in a sample of 13 Massai infants, those with easy temperaments were more likely to die (5/7) than those with difficult temperaments (1/6), something which is consistent with signals resulting in beneficial treatment (Vries 1987).

Crying does not always succeed in eliciting benefits, however. Compared to the previously mentioned groups, the Ngandu (Hewlett et al. 1998) and the Hadza (Blurton Jones 1993) have been found to be more likely to ignore the cries of infants and young children, and concerns over the risk of spoiling children by being too responsive seem to be reasonably common in some Western populations (Barnett et al. 2010; Burchinal, Skinner, and Reznick 2010; Chaudhary, Salali, and Swanepoel 2024; Frankenhuis and Amir 2022; Maute and Perren 2018). Crying can also lead to negative responses beyond being ignored (Barr 1990; Soltis 2004; Debra M. Zeifman and St James-Roberts 2017). It has been found to increase parental distress, the likelihood of maternal depression, and the child’s risk of maltreatment (Frodi 1985; Fujiwara et al. 2011; Howard et al. 2006; Lin and McFatter 2012; Soltis 2004). For example, in a sample of 3,259 parents of Dutch infants under 6 months old, 5.6% self-reported smothering, slapping, or shaking their babies in response to their crying, outcomes which were more likely among parents who were single, unemployed, or deemed their infant’s crying to be excessive (Reijneveld et al. 2004). Excessive infant crying, i.e., colic, has also been found to be a risk factor for multiple negative outcomes (Zeevenhooven et al. 2018), including abuse, shaken baby syndrome (Kurth et al. 2011), and earlier cessation of breastfeeding (Howard et al. 2006).

Although widely accepted as a signal, there is no consensus on why emotional crying may work to elicit positive responses. One possibility is that the physiological costs of crying for newborns and infants (Rao et al. 1997; Thureen et al. 1998) may be prohibitively costly for faking to pay off. When this is the case, crying may serve as a credible of vigor (Furlow 1997; Lummaa 1998; Soltis 2004; Wells 2003), something which may be particularly important early in life due to increased risk of infanticide for children who have has poor survival prospects, due to either health conditions or resource constraints (Daly and Wilson 1984; Soltis 2004).

For older children, the relatively insignificant energetic costs of crying may not be enough to result in credible signals in times of substantial conflicts of interest. For this reason, crying among older children may be more similar to adult crying, something which has been found to result in increased support and empathy but sometimes come at the cost of reputation and the potential for stigmatization (Cornelius and Labott 2001; Cretser et al. 1982; Hendriks and Vingerhoets 2006; Jesser 1989; Vingerhoets, Ven, and Velden 2016). Such costs may be a means by which crying can still be a credible signal (Sznycer, Gracanin, and Lieberman, n.d.) with moderate conflicts of interest if they dissuade potential fakers from making the signal.

Withdrawal

Withdrawal has been suggested to be an underappreciated way those with low social power may credibly signal need (Syme and Hagen 2023). Due to their lower status, children have limited ways to change how others treat them. However, as children 1) are a major component of parents’ inclusive fitness and 2) can help family members through allocare and other forms of labor, they provide benefits that are valuable and hard to replace. By withholding these benefits or putting the future of these benefits into question, a child simultaneously delays the benefits they get from this cooperation while imposing costs on those with a fitness interest in the child. As these costs are likely lower for those truly in need than those who are not, withdrawal may be a credible way to signal need if the costs to faking the signal make it unprofitable. In these cases, the end result is analogous to a labor strike, in which the signaler conveys to others their situation demands more support, and if they want to continue receiving benefits from the child, they need to upregulate their support of the child.

One of the key proximate mechanisms behind withdrawal is sadness or low mood (Scherer and Wallbott 1994)[[3]](#_ftn3). Sadness is a type of negative affect that is generally thought to arise from loss (Horwitz, Wakefield, and Lorenzo-Luaces 2017; Levenson 1999; Smith and Lazarus 1993), especially when one has little power to reverse or mitigate the loss on one’s own (Emanuel and Eldar 2023; Gunderson et al. 2021). Sadness and low mood, like other forms of psychological pain, probably evolved to motivate victims of adversity to shift their attention to the causes of adversity to mitigate its negative fitness consequences and to learn to solve or avoid future such adverse events (Andrews and Thomson 2009; Nesse 1990; Thornhill and Thornhill 1989)[[MG12]](#_msocom_12). Low mood, for example, is often thought to be adaptive in situations when activity is dangerous or unlikely to result in benefits, with anhedonia, pessimism, and fatigue all helping reduce behavior that may not be worth the costs or that may cause additional problems (Nesse 2019; Nettle 2009). It may also encourage the reappraisal of one’s circumstances after new information comes to light that one’s current strategy is not working (Nesse and Ellsworth 2009).

Through the reduction in behavior and attention in one’s usual interests that can accompany sadness and low-mood (Nettle 2009), one’s relatives and other social partners would likely gain some indication, in the form of cues, that the sad individual was experiencing adversity and could benefit from increased support. In addition to this cue-based information, sadness also results in facial expressions and crying, both of which probably evolved to indicate need (Balsters et al. 2013; Bowlby 1980; Reed and DeScioli 2017; Darwin 1872). Such expressions seem to be important to conveying this information as is seen by responses to those who cannot produce certain elements of facial expressions like tear production (Hasson 2009; Nesse 2019) or certain facial movements (Bogart, Tickle-Degnen, and Joffe 2012; Bogart and Matsumoto 2010). However, facial expressions, along with tears, may not be enough to credibly signal need in times of significant conflicts of interest. In these instances, depression and suicidal behavior have been suggested to function as ways to credibly signal need due to the expectation that such states are costlier for those facing adversity than those who are not (for more detail on these approaches see: (Edward H. Hagen 2003; Rosenthal 1993; Syme, Garfield, and Hagen 2016; Watson and Andrews 2002; Gaffney et al. 2022).

Temper tantrums and self-harm

Temper tantrums may also be adaptive strategies by which children can elicit more support from parents. They are common in children from 18 months to 4 years old (Potegal and Davidson 2003), and similar behaviors have also been reported in other young animals, often surrounding feeding or weaning conflict (Altmann 1980; Goodall 1986; Schaller 1964). Temper tantrums vary in their severity, ranging from crying, shrieking, and screaming to violent bodily movements, aggression, and self-harm (Potegal and Davidson 2003). They have been reported in many societies with different subsistence systems, political organization, and norms surrounding anger and parenting (Albino and Thompson 1956; Bani Salameh et al. 2021; Fouts, Hewlett, and Lamb 2005; Henderson 1966; Hilger 1957; Konner 1974; Ward 1970), suggesting they may be a human universal. As with the other proposed signals of need, they are capable of eliciting a wide range of responses, including pro-child responses, neutral responses (e.g., parents ignoring the child) (Hilger 1957; Konner 1974), and negative responses like abuse (Schmitt 1987).

As with the vocalizations that come with crying, temper tantrums might motivate caretakers to upregulate their support of the signaler due to the energetic costs of tantrums or the increased risk of predation stemming from the noisy display, both of which might result in credible signals if they are costly enough to not be worth faking (Lummaa 1998). In addition, tantrums also impose direct costs on caretakers who may be less able to do fitness enhancing tasks (Altmann 1980) like finding or preparing food or taking care of other children.

Another possibility is that temper tantrums carry the risk or implication of offspring self-harm. For example, Utilian mothers reported in focus groups that some temper tantrums involved children banging their heads on things when they do not get what they want, something that has been reported in other populations (Belden, Thomson, and Luby 2008; Burrows and Spiro 1953; Daniels, Mandleco, and Luthy 2012; Dennis 1940; Van den akker, Hoffenaar, and Overbeek 2022). Similarly, young chimpanzees (Pan troglodytes) have been observed to beat their heads on the ground when refused access to breast milk (Goodall 1986), and American white pelican chicks (Pelecanus erythrorhynchos) have been found to throw themselves on the ground, flail violently, and bite at their own wings when begging for food (Schaller 1964). Such behaviors have been suggested to follow similar logic to depression and suicide attempts as they are likely relatively less costly to those who are truly in dire need compared to those who are not to the point faking is not worth the costs (Edward H. Hagen, Watson, and Hammerstein 2008). In other words, the risk of injury from violent temper tantrums carries greater fitness costs to a child who is receiving enough support to develop high levels of embodied capital than it does to a child who is neglected to the point where their survival prospects are low. In this scenario, the risk of harm is likely not worth the potential benefits of increased support to the child receiving substantial investment, but it may be for the neglected child who may have few other options to compel others to invest in them.

Study Aims

This study was designed to help establish the natural history of child signaling behavior in Utila, Honduras, by quantifying the frequencies of common child signals of need and various types of parental responses. From this, we sought to use signaling theory to examine 1) the causes of child signals of need, 2) how and why signal costs might vary, 3) how parents respond to indications of child need, 4) how signaling behavior changes with age and sex, 5) and if there were differences in child signaling and parental responses two groups who face different levels of social and economic adversity.

A major challenge when studying signals of need is the fact that such signaling is expected to 1) be more common in times when individuals can benefit from increased investment (e.g., when they are facing major adversity or when a fitness-relevant opportunity arises) and 2) result in benefits to the signaler on average. This hypothisized causal, bi-directional relationship between investment and signaling makes it difficult to isolate the effects of signaling behavior on investment in children when using standard measures of child welfare (i.e., BMI, health metrics, and height for age). When experiments are not appropriate, longitudinal studies with carefully selected variables can help address this (Arjas and Parner 2004). However, our small sample size with signaling data and anthropometric data at two time periods limits our ability to do this in this study. For this reason, all analyses for this study were exploratory and meant to inform future studies of child signaling.

Methods

Study population

This study was done in Utila, Honduras, one of the country’s three major Bay Islands in the Western Caribbean. Participants in the study largely fit into two groups who face different levels of economic and social adversity, 1) a long-standing English-speaking community primarily made up of the descendants of expats and 2) a relatively new community of Spanish-speaking immigrants from the mainland. Compared to those born on Utila, immigrants from the mainland (Davis 2014) are more likely to live in crowded and unsanitary conditions, face active discrimination (A. R. Garcia, Gurven, and Blackwell 2017), and experience economic uncertainty due the predominance of low paying, seasonal jobs primarily related to the island’s diving tourism industry (A. Garcia 2018; Stonich 2000). Such inequality appears to be increasing in response to recent growth in the tourism industry, with the majority of wealth from tourism being enjoyed by a small portion of expat islanders and westerners (Davis 2014).

During focus groups held in July 2022, parents indicated high levels of sibling competition and various ways children might signal need. Both Spanish-speaking and English-speaking moms reported that fighting between siblings was extremely common, with fights over toys, gifts, and phone time being the most reported causes. In both populations, crying and various forms of withdrawal were listed as the most common responses to a child not receiving investment or support, followed by temper tantrums and making messes in their home. Spanish-speaking mothers reported that refusing to do homework was a common tactic, while English-speaking mothers emphasized running away and self-harm (e.g., a child slamming their head on the ground) to a greater degree. In response to these hypothesized signals, discipline was common. Although the mothers generally reported that children were treated equally, needier children were described as getting more resources from parents, something that may be partially mediated by differences in signaling among children in addition to information caretakers gained from cues.

Survey

The survey data for this study come from a larger project run by the Utila Child Health Project. Data was collected by either Jess Hlay, Izabel Rodriguez, Daniela Mejia, Angela Garcia, or Caroline Smith who read the survey questions to participants and recorded their answers. Participants could choose to do the survey in English or Spanish.

Frequency of child signaling, alloparenting contributions, and conflict with caretaker

We used 6-point Likert scales to get caregiver reports on the frequencies of 1) various types of child signaling, 2) interpersonal conflict with each participating child, 3) and child alloparenting behavior. See Table 1 for exact wording. Caretakers were often not asked about child alloparenting behavior if a child was an only child. For this reason, we gave children an alloparenting frequency of never if they were the only child within a household.

As these responses reflect frequencies of behavior, we converted these to counts which reflected the number of times per month a child engaged in these behaviors for analyses. The original wording of the Likert scale and the frequency conversion are shown in Table 2.

From these count variables, we created three additional signaling variables to be used, primarily, as our main dependent variables in analyses. The frequency of signaling was the sum of the frequencies of child sadness, crying, and temper tantrums. Maximum signal frequency was the frequency of the most common of these three signals. Signal cost was the sum of the frequencies of these three signals weighted by their relative costliness (i.e., Signal cost = frequency of sadness + frequency of crying * 2 + frequency of temper tantrums * 3). Although we collected data on the frequency of running away, it was not included in the creation of any of these variables due to uncertainty over whether it was likely to be a credible signal due to the relative safety of Utila compared to other areas in which running away has been found to be a successful strategy for children.

We also calculated the summed frequency of child alloparenting contributions by other children within the household.

Causes and responses to the last instance of child signaling

We asked caretakers about the last time each participating child was sad or cried. This involved asking what the caretaker thought caused the emotion or display and recording their answer in a text box. We then asked how the parent responded to their child. The researcher giving the survey would then bin responses into at least one of 31 categories which included positive, negative, and neutral responses (from the child’s perspective). For the full list of options see Table S1.

From this data, we created a parental response variable, which was coded as 1 for a positive response, 0 for a neutral response, and -1 for a negative response (all from the child’s perspective). Statistical analyses

All statistical analyses were exploratory and involved fitting many models with different combinations of independent and dependent variables. While were cognizant of the risk of overfitting the data, it is still more likely than if the study was entirely confirmatory (Tukey 1977). The payoff to this approach is the identification of theoretically relevant effects that should be tested in the future.

We fit generalized linear mixed models for exploratory analyses with signal frequencies and summed signal cost as dependent variables. As our signaling variables involved over-dispersed count data, we used negative binomial regression. This was done in R with the GLMM package (Brooks et al. 2017), with household as a random effect. Control variables differed in some models, but child age and sex were always included. Specific control variables are mentioned in the results.

For the models examining 1) parental responses to the last reported time each participating child was sad or cried, 2) the relative neediness of the child, and 3) the investment a child received compared to the other children in the household, we fit ordinal logistic regression models using two methods. The first used the polr function in the MASS package (Venables and Ripley 2002). The results were then interpreted with clustered standard errors for household (Zeileis and Hothorn 2002). Due to the risk of overfitting that comes with having too few clusters (Cameron and Miller 2015), we also fit the same models using BRMS (Bürkner 2017) with household as a random effect.

Discussion

Negative association between age and child signaling (all signaling measures)

As seen in Western cultures (Debra M. Zeifman 2013; Potegal and Davidson 2003; Koch 2003) (check for others), the frequencies of crying and temper tantrums decreased with age, something which is likely due to numerous reasons. One possibility is that older children have greater ability to satisfy one’s desires and behave in ways which match cultural expectations of teenagers and adults. Another is that since one’s reproductive value increases as one survives periods of high mortality risk in childhood, the intensity of conflicts of interest with parents decreases since these children are likely to reach adulthood and require less investment to reach adulthood than younger children, current or future (Daly and Wilson 1995). For this reason, less costly signals may be more likely to work to increase investment, all else being equal[[4]]. That said, all else may often not be equal if investment in older children is costlier, something which may be reflected in caretakers seeing older children as relatively needy. The decrease in crying and tantrum frequencies with age may also result from the potential for greater reputational costs, something which may also increase the credibility of such signals among older children as previously mentioned. It is also possible that parents see older children less than younger children. However, the negative association between need with age observed in other societies and the fact that all children in this study were of school age suggest that this is unlikely to be the sole reason responsible for this.

There was no significant association between the frequency of child sadness and age. Part of this likely results from the fact that, unlike crying, sadness is thought to have important non-signaling functions including drawing one’s attention to a problem, motivating deeper analysis of one’s situation, and causing withdrawal from situations with low fitness prospects (Nesse 2019; Nettle 2009; Allen and Badcock 2003; Edward H. Hagen 2011), from which, others can still obtain cue-based information about one’s emotional state, well-being, and desires. By asking about sadness as a whole, our measure was unable to disentangle information caretakers gleaned from cues from information that came from signals. For this reason, a change in signals that arise from sadness outside of crying (e.g., facial sadness and some, but all, forms of withdrawal) may have been harder to detect with our sample size even if young children expressed facial sadness more often. It is also possible that there is no effect of age on sadness frequency among the age ranges examined it.

Positive association between conflict with parent and child signaling (all signaling measures)

Consistent with signaling models which emphasize the role of conflicts of interest in favoring the use of costlier signals, there were positive associations between caretakers’ perceptions of conflict frequency and 1) the summed costliness of children’s signaling, 2) the frequency of child signals of need, and 3) the frequency of children’s most common need signaling strategy. Although conflicts of interest do not always lead to behavioral conflict (Altmann 1980; Bateson 1994), it is likely many, if not most, conflicts are ultimately caused by conflicts of interest, making it a useful proxy for the underlying conflicts of interest that may increase the benefits of deception and the costs of being deceived[[5]]. If this is the case, the association between signaling and conflict may represent children’s attempts at credibly signaling their value of additional investment or the costliness of punishment (Syme and Hagen 2019) to parents who would have good reason to doubt the trustworthiness of simple verbal requests (or the trustworthiness of facial sadness and crying for those resorting to temper tantrums).

Sex and focal child alloparenting effort interact (positive for females, negative/flat for males)

We found evidence of an interaction between how often a child cared for their siblings and the sex of the child in our models examining signal frequencies and cost, whereby there was a positive relationship between the frequency and summed costliness of child signals of need and alloparenting effort for girls but no relationship or a slightly negative one for boys. In many societies, girls are often expected to do more alloparenting within the household and often end up doing so Weisner and Gallimore (1977). While girls in this sample were also reported to give more allocare, the difference was not significant. However, Utilian mothers reported in focus groups (confirm) that girls tend to provide more help for their siblings than boys. If this difference was the outcome of caretakers pressuring girls to do more or resulted in the expectation of more alloparenting, the increase in signaling with alloparenting effort seen among girls may be the result of a strategy to signal the costliness of their efforts in order to invest more in romantic relationships and status seeking behaviors outside the household.

Evidence for conflicts similar to the one hypothesized here has been reported by multiple researchers working with the Chuukese. Lowe found that both male and female children reported conflict with their families over their desires to invest financial resources in their own status enhancing opportunities rather than in the household (Lowe 2003). In an analysis of conflict among Chuukese-Americans, Syme and Hagen found that severe conflicts were more likely with greater numbers of siblings and when conflicts involved a child spending time away from home or evading family responsibilities (Syme and Hagen 2023), findings which would be consistent with parents pressuring older children into investing more in the household among other explanations. In response to such conflicts, participants reported using a wide range strategies as children including, deception, withholding cooperation, running away, and seeking the assistance of others. Through these means, participants were often able to resolve conflicts in their favor despite their lower status in the household and community.

Body fat discussion

Children with less body fat cried more, threw more temper tantrums, and had higher summed signal costs. In other words, needier children signaled more often. Although the body fat by sex interaction was only marginally significant, our models predict a negative relationship between body fat and signaling for girls and no relationship for boys. Female reproduction requires a significant caloric invest in children, both in the womb and when breastfeeding (Jasienska 2009), costs which males do not have to pay. For this reason, low body fat, which can prevent ovulation in extreme cases (Ellison 2003), is likely a be better predictor of future reproductive success in adolescent (and older) girls than boys, potentially making signaling for increased investment a more viable strategy in instances when an individual has low body fat.

Positive associations between child age and signaling frequency and child neediness

There was a positive association between the summed frequency of child signaling and caretaker perceptions of child neediness within the family, something which would be consistent with sadness, crying, and temper tantrums being signals (or involving signals in the case of crying) if parents took this to mean how much they value additional investment. Caretakers also perceived older children as needier, despite having more conflicts with younger children. One reason for this could be that caretakers weighted monetary investment highly when evaluating child need.

Signaling behavior did not predict (try more models?) differences in relative caregiver investment between children within households. However, crying frequency was associated with both height and weight gain for older children. If older children face greater reputation costs from crying, crying may be a relatively credible credible signal compared to crying among younger children, with it being a less viable strategy to exaggerate one’s value of investment or the costliness of adversity.

Parental responses to last signal of need

Parents were more likely to respond positively and less likely to respond negatively to the last time a child signaled when the cause involved discomfort, pain, injury, or illness than when it did not. One potential causal explanation for this is that parents may often have information for sources outside of child signaling to corroborate a child’s need (e.g., a fever, a diagnosis, or witness a child hurt themselves) so that there is less private information about a child’s current state. Simultaneously, conflicts of interest may be less significant in these times due to either 1) both sides benefiting similarly from their child returning to normal (e.g., with an illness) or 2) the cause involving something which is minor and would quickly resolve itself (e.g. a skinned knee). It may also be that many of the typical positive responses to pain and illness are not very costly to caretakers (e.g., providing comfort), thereby increasing their frequency.

When the cause involved punishment, however, parents were more likely to respond negatively and less likely to respond positively. In these circumstances, children may be signaling about the severity of a punishment (Syme and Hagen 2019). However, parents may be reluctant to reduce punishments if the behavior that warranted the punishment was repeated or severe. They may also react with scolding or further punishment. That said, our model predicts positive responses in 37% of cases (when all other predictors are held at their mean values), suggesting that children are successful in negotiating better outcomes at least some of the time.

Although not a significant relationship, the negative effect of the family conflict being a reported cause of signaling on the likelihood of a positive response may reflect tradeoffs within the family, whereby investment in one child cannot go to another. Caretakers may have also responded more negatively when the cause involved family conflict, if it involved a child imposing costs on other family members.

In most models, there is a negative association between education and child signaling?

We found a negative association between caretakers’ education level (years in school) and many of our child signaling measures despite our models controlling for household income and partner status. One possible reason for this may be that more educated caretakers see their children less during the day due to greater ability to afford childcare or due to them having jobs that are less compatible with child care (test model of social support predicted by education). Unfortunately, we do not have data on how much time caretakers spent with children to test this, and it seems equally likely that less educated parents may see their kids less often if they need to work more hours to support their families. Another possibility is that lower levels of education are associated with greater levels of adversity for both parents and children, something which appears to be the case for other measures of SES (Pepper and Nettle 2017) and would increase the benefits of signaling need for children and the difficulty of parents to respond. It is also possible that education influences parenting norms in ways that cause differences in child signaling behavior.

Lack of evidence for demographic effects

Outside of the sex by alloparenting effort interaction, we found little evidence of sex differences in child signaling behavior. As adults, females cry more than males, a finding that has been reported across cultures (Hemert, Vijver, and Vingerhoets 2011; Becht, Poortinga, and Vingerhoets 2001). One reason why our study may have failed to find significant effects of sex is that sex differences in the causes and reputational effects of signaling need are small or nonexistent until adolescence and young adulthood, with our small sample size for this age range preventing us from detecting a real effect should it exist. It is also possible that the sex difference seen in rates of crying in adults may reflect the lesser ability of females to bargain for better treatment through aggression or the threat of it when faced with adversity (Edward H. Hagen and Rosenström 2016). Such a sex difference may not exist among preadolescencents due to a lack of difference in strength or athletic ability beofre puberty (Handelsman, Hirschberg, and Bermon 2018) or due to their relative lack of formidability compared to adults.

Monthly income was also not found to be a significant predictor of signaling behavior. Many models designed to explain signals of need (e.g., the begging of young birds) predict that offspring who are worse off or are receiving less investment have more to gain from signaling than those with more (Mock, Dugas, and Strickler 2011; Godfray 1991; Caro et al. 2016; Godfray and Johnstone 2000). However, it is possible that this can be true and household level resources have little influence on child signaling frequencies between families. As signaling behavior is costly to child, caretakers, and siblings, children may gain direct and indirect fitness benefits from limiting their signaling behavior in times of resource stress (i.e., when the chances of gaining investment or the size of investment are small). Similarly, children in high resource families may also be motivated to signal at high frequencies due to the potential for larger pieces of investment. We know of no empirical explorations of this in humans (check more and confirm). However, multiple game theoretical models examining parent-offspring conflict have found that parental resources have a modest impact on the evolutionary stable value for offspring demand for resources (Bossan, Hammerstein, and Koehncke 2013; Mock and Parker 1997 [confirm]). Although counterintuitive, such an outcome fits the fact that fitness is relative and that natural selection should be expected to have favored those who maximized their own inclusive fitness, whether one found themselves in a great situation or a dire one. Compared to overall effort in eliciting need, resource availability may have a larger impact on how children signal for more investment based on the finding that birds in stable and unusually favorable environments are more likely to compete with siblings through signals thought to reveal information about need, while those in relatively unpredictable and unusually poor environments have been found to compete more through signals thought to reveal information about quality (Caro et al. 2016).

Despite population level differences in wealth, job certainty, discrimination, and living conditions (Davis 2014; A. R. Garcia, Gurven, and Blackwell 2017; A. Garcia 2018; Stonich 2000), we generally did not find an effect of population on child signaling behavior. Although we sought data from both English-speaking participants born on the island and Spanish-speaking immigrants from the mainland, our smaller sample of English-speaking Utilians may have limited our ability to detect real differences between the populations. It is possible that differences in wealth and status between the groups translate to differences in child signaling due to children facing different types adversity or weighting different types of investment differently. It is also likely that the two groups differ in how caretakers could respond to child signaling, and caretakers between the two populations may also differ in their cultural models of parenting. However, as previously mentioned, it is conceivable that household level differences have little effect on child signaling behavior.

Limitations

The main limitation of this study is that it is completely exploratory, something which results in a high risk of our models overfitting the data and indicating associations which might not exist in the real world. Our study was also predominantly cross-sectional, further limiting our ability to make causal inferences.

Our use of convenience and snowball sampling is also a limitation as it resulted in a non-probability sample which may not be representative of the broader population. This could happen if there was selection bias so that those who participated differed from the rest of the population in how they interacted with their children or how they assessed, remembered, and reported child behavior.

Outside of child anthropometric measurements, all data come from caregiver reports, and therefore, reflect the biases of caretakers, whatever they may be. Due to the fact the data used here were a part of survey which covered many topics and often multiple children, we also faced a tradeoff between survey time and question depth. Asking adult participants about their relative investment in children in their household was likely not as effective as more in depth, questions focused on specific questions. Framing the question this way may have also increased the likelihood of social desirability bias (Grimm 2010), which could lead adult participants to be be more likely to report equal investment across children. Our questions about the last time the child signaled may have also been more effective if they focused on specific signals due to our methods combining crying, which is unambiguous as a signal, with sadness, which has both signaling and non-signaling functions.
